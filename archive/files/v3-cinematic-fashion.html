Chapter Two · The Pattern

A builder in product manager clothing.

The pattern in Shivam Sharma's career resists usual product-management résumé arithmetic, which prefers titles, headcount, and stage. The actual signal is this: at nineteen he founded a marketing agency that grew to serve two hundred clients across the United Kingdom and the Middle East. At twenty-two he co-founded a real-estate CRM automation business that managed a two-hundred-thousand-dollar marketing budget and influenced more than five million dollars in closed deals. By twenty-four, both ventures had been acquired — combined exit five hundred and seventy-five thousand dollars, ninety-five percent retention post-acquisition, no outside capital ever taken.

What followed was the kind of career-collapse most founders dread and few execute deliberately: a master's program in AI in Business at Simon, completed in eighteen months, STEM-certified. A founding-PM stint at Vollie shipping a voice-AI coaching platform from zero to one as the sole product owner. A product-lead role at the Ain Center where he replaced a twenty-five-thousand-dollar-per-year licensed simulator with VentureForge, an AI strategy sim grading fifty MBA decisions against real fuel prices and market shocks.

Then ESC Partners. The role title reads AI Product Manager; the job description reads engineer. The deliverable is Maya AI: a full operating layer on Oracle's Customer Care and Service product, built in two and a half months by a single person, currently live for two hundred thousand residents at the New England Power utility, with a supervisor cockpit running sentiment analysis on twenty-six-language voice traffic via 11Labs and one-click human takeover. Seventy to eighty percent of first-touch volume, automated. Two derivatives spun off: a standalone email triage agent at NEP, and VaultIQ — an air-gapped Claude Opus deployment inside the customer's VPC.
